DNS Website Checks

DNS tells browsers where your website lives. If DNS is confusing or inconsistent, people may have trouble reaching the right website or email service.

Quick FactDNS tells browsers and email providers where your website services live.

What is it?

DNS stands for Domain Name System. It connects a name like yourbusiness.com to the servers that host your website and email.

Most business owners do not need to manage DNS every day. But DNS is one of the basic systems your website depends on.

Why business owners care

DNS problems can make a website unavailable, send visitors to the wrong place, or make email harder to deliver. Even small record mistakes can create confusing customer experiences.

Good DNS records also make future website changes easier. They help your web designer, IT provider, or hosting company understand how the site is connected.

What Beacon reviews

Beacon reviews public DNS records for your domain. This includes common records for the website, email, verification, and ownership-related services.

Beacon does not access your registrar or hosting account. It only reviews information that is publicly available.

Where the information comes from

The information comes from public DNS lookups. These are the same records browsers, mail providers, and website tools use to find your services.

Beacon records what was visible at the time of review. DNS can change, so the date of the check matters.

How to read your report

A DNS note in your report usually means Beacon saw a record that deserves review or cleanup. It does not automatically mean something is broken.

The report should explain what the record appears to do and why it may matter. The goal is to help you ask the right question before changing anything.

Common mistakes

Common misunderstandingDo not treat one warning as the whole story. Read it with the rest of the report, then confirm the recommended change with the person who manages the website.

Recommended next step

Start by confirming who manages your domain and hosting. Then review any DNS recommendation with that provider before deleting or changing records.

Prioritize DNS issues that affect website availability, email delivery, or old services you no longer use.
