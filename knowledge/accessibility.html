Accessibility Website Checks

Accessibility checks help more people use your website successfully. They also make many websites clearer and easier for everyone.

Business ImpactClear labels, contrast and keyboard support help more customers use the site.

What is it?

Accessibility means designing and building a website so people with different abilities can use it. This includes people who use keyboards, screen readers, zoom, captions, or high contrast settings.

A Beacon review looks at practical, visible issues. It is not a full legal audit.

Why business owners care

Accessible websites are easier for more customers to use. They often have clearer labels, better contrast, and more predictable navigation.

Accessibility improvements can also reduce support friction. If people can complete tasks on the website, they are less likely to abandon the process.

What Beacon reviews

Beacon checks common issues such as missing image descriptions, unclear form labels, low contrast, heading order, focus visibility, and keyboard access. These are practical checks that often appear in automated tools and browser review.

Beacon explains what the issue means for a visitor rather than listing standards without context.

Where the information comes from

The information comes from public page markup, browser inspection, and automated checks such as Lighthouse accessibility tests. Some accessibility issues still require human judgment.

Beacon identifies what is visible in the review and recommends when manual review is appropriate.

How to read your report

An accessibility item usually points to a task that may be harder for some visitors. The report should explain the practical effect, such as a form field that is hard to understand.

Do not treat every warning as equal. Prioritize issues that block navigation, forms, purchases, or contact.

Common mistakes

Common misunderstandingDo not treat one warning as the whole story. Read it with the rest of the report, then confirm the recommended change with the person who manages the website.

Recommended next step

Fix issues that prevent people from reading, navigating, submitting forms, or contacting the business. Then improve lower-priority items during normal website updates.

Ask your web provider to retest key pages after changes.
