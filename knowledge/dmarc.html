DMARC Policy Explained

DMARC helps domain owners tell mail providers how to handle email that fails common trust checks. It is a policy layer that works with SPF and DKIM.

Common FixStart with monitoring before moving to stricter mail handling policies.

What is it?

DMARC stands for Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance. In plain English, it is a public instruction for mail providers.

A DMARC policy can ask providers to monitor, quarantine, or reject messages that do not pass the right checks.

Why business owners care

DMARC helps protect how your domain is used in email. It also gives your team a clearer path for improving email trust over time.

A strict policy should be set carefully. Moving too quickly can affect legitimate mail if SPF or DKIM is not ready.

What Beacon reviews

Beacon checks whether a DMARC record exists and what policy it declares. Beacon also looks for reporting addresses and basic syntax issues.

Beacon does not read private email reports or access your mailbox. It reviews the public DMARC record only.

Where the information comes from

The information comes from a public DNS TXT record for DMARC.

Mail providers use this record when checking incoming messages.

Beacon records what was visible during the review and explains the policy in plain English.

How to read your report

A DMARC result may show that your domain is monitoring mail, partially enforcing a policy, or not publishing a policy. None of those results should be judged without context.

The report should help you understand whether the policy matches your current email setup and business tolerance for change.

Common mistakes

Common misunderstandingDo not treat one warning as the whole story. Read it with the rest of the report, then confirm the recommended change with the person who manages the website.

Recommended next step

Confirm SPF and DKIM before tightening DMARC. Then move policy changes gradually with help from your email provider or IT partner.

If no DMARC record exists, start with a monitoring policy and review what mail services are actually sending for your domain.
